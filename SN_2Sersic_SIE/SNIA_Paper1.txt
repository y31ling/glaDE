ABSTRACT
The ﬁrst multiply-lensed Supernova Type Ia (SN Ia) discovered, iPTF16geu, formed an Einstein cross
displaying a broad range of image magniﬁcations. Constrained by its image positions, all previous
lens models that employ a simple smoothly-varying mass distribution have failed to reproduce the
broad range of image magniﬁcations; moreover, lens models that employ two smoothly-varying mass
components (representing stars and Dark Matter separately) require oﬀset centres just to best ﬁt the
image positions, while only modestly increasing the range of predicted image magniﬁcations. Here,
by isolating and subtracting away the Einstein ring formed by the SN Ia host galaxy, the lensing
galaxy is revealed as a barred spiral. Constrained again by the SN Ia image positions, lens models
that incorporate a model stellar component that ﬁts the light morphology of the lensing galaxy, along
with a simple power-law proﬁle for a DM halo sharing the same centre as the stellar component,
leave no position anomalies and now predict a broader range of image magniﬁcations in better accord
with observations. The best-matching lens model closely or satisfactorily reproduces the disparate
magniﬁcations of the two brightest SN Ia images, albeit over-predicting (by a factor of ∼2.5) the
comparable magniﬁcations of the two dimmest images. This work sets the stage for a more complete
assessment of lensing anomalies left by smooth lens models for iPTF16geu – crucial for evaluating
what additional ingredients are needed to resolve these anomalies, with corresponding implications for
the nature of Dark Matter.
1. INTRODUCTION
iPTF16geu is the ﬁrst Supernova Type Ia (SN Ia) ver-
iﬁed to be gravitationally lensed into multiple images
(Goobar et al. 2017). Figure 1 shows images of this sys-
tem taken at two diﬀerent epochs by the Hubble Space
Telescope (HST). In the ﬁrst epoch (Fig. 1a), the SN Ia
can be seen to be lensed into four unresolved images,
which are labelled Img 1 to Img 4 clockwise from the
brightest image. All four SN Ia images are superposed
on an Einstein ring (ER) corresponding to the multiply-
lensed images of the SN Ia host galaxy. The morphol-
ogy of this ER can be seen at greater clarity in the im-
age taken more than 2 years later (Fig. 1b), by which
time the SN Ia had faded to obscurity. The ER encir-
cles a visible lensing galaxy, which has a diﬀerent colour
than both the SN Ia and the ER. Utilised as a stan-
dard candle, this SN Ia permits, for the ﬁrst time, a
determination of the magniﬁcation imposed upon each
image of a multiply-lensed system, rather than just the
relative magniﬁcations of multiply-lensed images when
the luminosity of the lensed object is unknown. Lens
models for iPTF16geu must therefore satisfy more strin-
gent criteria than is normally the case for multiply-
lensed systems – making it, along with the other reasons
mentioned below, a particularly noteworthy system for
studying lensing anomalies. The latter corresponds to
diﬀerences left by lens models between the predicted and
observed positions and (absolute or relative) magniﬁca-
tions of multiply-lensed systems. The solution to lensing
anomalies can have important implications for the na-
ture of Dark Matter (DM), as has been demonstrated
for multiply-lensed quasars (Amruth et al. 2023).

Lens modelling of iPTF16geu began by adopting just
a single smoothly-varying density proﬁle for the lens-
ing galaxy1.
Constrained solely by the SN Ia image
positions, all such lens models are reportedly able to
reproduce the SN Ia image positions2, but predict a
smaller range in SN Ia image magniﬁcations – no larger
than a factor of ∼2, and sometimes much less – com-
pared to that observed. Speciﬁcally, adopting a singu-
lar isothermal ellipsoid (power-law proﬁle with an index
of α = 2, whereby the density, n, decreases with ra-
dius, r, as n ∝ r−α), both Goobar et al. (2017) and
More et al. (2017) ﬁnd that their lens models predict
relatively small diﬀerences in magniﬁcations (within a
factor of ∼2) among all four SN Ia images. Even when
allowing the density proﬁle of the model ellipsoid to dif-
fer from isothermal (α ≠ 2), More et al. (2017) still ﬁnd
a best-ﬁt density proﬁle close to isothermal irrespective
of whether external shear is imposed. Adopting instead
cored power-law ellipsoids, M¨ortsell et al. (2020) ﬁnd
a best-ﬁt lens model having a density proﬁle shallower
than isothermal, and an oﬀset between the lens and light
centres of ∼0.1 kpc (as can be seen in their Fig. 8, al-
though the oﬀset value quoted is from Williams & Zeg-
eye 2020). Even then, their lens model predicts quite
comparable magniﬁcations (well within a factor of 2)
for all four SN Ia images. Similarly, adopting a S´ersic
proﬁle, Dhawan et al. (2020) ﬁnd that their lens mod-
els (having S´ersic indices, nS, of nS ≈ 1) predicts quite
comparable magniﬁcations (again within a factor of 2)
for all four SN Ia images3.
Subsequent lens models adopted multiple mass com-
ponents for the lensing galaxy in iPTF16geu, with each
component varying smoothly in density with radius.
Once again, when constrained solely by the SN Ia im-
age positions, all such lens models are reportedly able to
reproduce the SN Ia image positions but not the SN Ia
image magniﬁcations – albeit spanning a larger range
of magniﬁcations than the aforementioned lens mod-
els employing only a single mass component. Speciﬁ-
cally, Williams & Zegeye (2020) considered the superpo-
sition of two cored ellipsoids (forced to closely resemble
spheroids) having diﬀerent scale sizes (the smaller rep-
resenting stars and the larger representing a DM halo),

Even such complex lens models are far from
being able to reproduce the observed SN Ia image mag-

in this paper, Diego et al. (2022) incorporated a stellar
component based on a composite image of the lensing
galaxy (after removing the ER) constructed from images
taken in the F814W and F160W ﬁlters by the HST, to-

SN Ia image positions, the best-ﬁt lens model requires
the orientation of the DM component (the cored ellip-
soid) to closely match that of the stellar image (at its
outer regions), but for the centre of the DM component
to be oﬀset from the centre of the stellar component by
∼0.23 kpc. This lens model predicts magniﬁcations that
disagree with those observed for Img 1, Img 2, and Img 4,
such that Img 1 (the brightest image) only has a slightly
higher predicted magniﬁcation than Img 4 (formally the
second-dimmest image). Using the magniﬁcations of se-
lected images as additional constraints can help improve
the overall match in magniﬁcations, but at predicted po-
sitions diﬀerent from the observed positions.
Failure in reproducing all four SN Ia image magniﬁ-
cations have stimulated studies exploring whether the
addition of stellar micro-lensing – by stars in the fore-
ground lensing galaxy – can potentially resolve the mag-
niﬁcation anomalies left by smooth lens models. Adopt-
ing the smooth lens model constructed by More et al.
(2017) as their macro-lens (an ellipsoid), Yahalomi et al.
(2017) determine a probability of ≲ 3% for stellar micro-
lensing to perturb the model-predicted magniﬁcations
at the level necessary to match the observed magniﬁca-
tions, even when allowing the ellipticity and shear of the
macro-lens to vary. By contrast, utilising a single cored
ellipsoid as the macro-lens, M¨ortsell et al. (2020) ﬁnd
a maximal probability of ∼12% for stellar micro-lensing
to perturb the magniﬁcations of the SN Ia images suf-
ﬁciently to match the observed magniﬁcations. Based
instead on their macro-lens employing a stellar compo-
nent together with a cored ellipsoid, Diego et al. (2022)
found a probability of ∼10% for stellar micro-lensing to
reproduce the magniﬁcation of the SN Ia image that is
most severely under-predicted by their macro-lens model
(Img 1). Both M¨ortsell et al. (2020) and Diego et al.
(2022) suggest that stellar micro-lensing is responsible
for the magniﬁcation anomalies left by their smooth lens
models for iPTF16geu.

Unlike multiply-lensed systems involving quasi-stellar
objects (QSOs) in which the lensing galaxy is often
diﬃcult to discern against the glare of a multiply-
lensed QSO, supernovae eventually fade to obscurity. In
iPTF16geu, we demonstrate how a simple procedure to
remove the ER in the post-SN Ia image of iPTF16geu
(see Section 2) reveals the lensing galaxy in this sys-
tem to be a barred spiral, as shown in Figure 2. The
four SN Ia images form near the periphery of the stel-
lar bar, as indicated in Figure 2b, which along with the
stellar disk imposes a lensing potential that is highly
asymmetric. We ﬁt two elliptical S´ersic proﬁles to the
light morphology of the lensing galaxy to serve as a
model stellar component for this galaxy. Employing this
model stellar component along with diﬀerent choices of
commonly-used density proﬁles for a DM halo, and con-
strained by just the SN Ia image positions alone (like
in previous studies), our lens models predict a broader
range of SN Ia image magniﬁcations that more closely
resemble those observed, while oftentimes reproducing
near exactly the SN Ia image positions. Within the ER,
4
our lens models require the projected stellar mass to
be comparable to the projected DM mass, so that the
stellar component strongly inﬂuences the lensing deﬂec-
tion ﬁeld at the ER. Unlike the lens models proposed by
Williams & Zegeye (2020) and Diego et al. (2022), who
also employed multiple mass components, our lens mod-
els do not demand the stellar and DM components to
be oﬀset from each other – thus requiring very complex
total mass distributions of questionable physical reality
– so as to match the SN Ia image positions. Our best-ﬁt
lens model near-exactly reproduces the magniﬁcation of
Img 2 (second-brightest image), comes quite close to re-
producing the magniﬁcation of Img 1 (brightest image)
by leaving just a 2.15σ tension, although over-predicting
the magniﬁcations of Img 3–4 (the two dimmest images
having comparable brightnesses) by factors of between
2 and 3. This lens model therefore leaves no individ-
ual position anomalies and alleviates, albeit still leaves,
magniﬁcation anomalies.
This paper is the ﬁrst of a series to carefully evalu-
ate how closely smooth lens models are able to repro-
duce the observed properties of multiply-lensed images
in iPTF16geu, and the nature of additional ingredients
needed to resolve any lensing anomalies left over by such
lens models. For example, because the eﬀects of stellar
micro-lensing depend on the macro-lens model adopted,
our more exacting smooth lens models may signiﬁcantly
alter the probability for stellar micro-lensing to perturb
the magniﬁcations of the SN Ia images suﬃciently to re-
produce their observed magniﬁcations.
Alternatively,
an entirely diﬀerent ingredient may be needed to re-
solve lensing anomalies left over by smooth lens models
for iPTF16geu, such as DM sub-halos as are predicted
in abundance if DM comprises ultra-massive and cold
particles (e.g., WIMPs), or pervasive density modula-
tions in DM halos generated by quantum interference
among ultra-light bosons in a Bose-Einstein condensate
(e.g., ultra-light Axion-like particles). Both WIMPs and
Axions have been advocated to address diﬀerent deﬁ-
ciencies in the Standard Model of particle physics, and
ultra-light Axion-like particles advocated by string the-
ory; at the present time, these particles constitute the
main contenders for DM. In lieu of a direct detection
in laboratory experiments, the type (position or mag-
niﬁcation, or both) and magnitude of lensing anomalies
left by smooth lens models, and the resolution to such
anomalies, oﬀer a promising approach to diﬀerentiate
between the main contenders for DM (e.g., see Amruth
et al. 2023).
The remainder of this paper is organised as follows. In
Section 2, we explain how we separate the ER from the
lensing galaxy in iPTF16geu, thus revealing the lens-
ing galaxy in this system as a barred spiral. We then
provide details on the two elliptical S´ersic proﬁles ﬁtted
to the light morphology of the lensing galaxy to serve
as a model stellar component for this galaxy. In Sec-
tion 3, we explain the ingredients incorporated into our
lens models for iPTF16geu, both including and exclud-
ing the model stellar component so as to assess its eﬀects
on the predicted properties of the SN Ia images. Like in
previous studies, we use only the SN Ia image positions
as constraints, measured in the manner described also
in Section 3.
In Section 4, we point out how simply
evaluating lensing anomalies alone may not provide an
accurate assessment of how well lens models are able to
reproduce the properties of multiply-lensed images; to
remedy this problem, we advocate an additional diag-
nostic that ought to be provided when reporting lens
models. Then, in Section 5, we present the predictions
of our lens models, and demonstrate the importance of
the model stellar component, as well as the necessity of
a DM halo, for signiﬁcantly improving upon the match
in both the SN Ia image positions and magniﬁcations.
Finally, in Section 6, we summarise the main points of
this paper and forecast the issues to be tackled in fu-
ture papers of this series. We adopt throughout a con-
cordance ΛCDM cosmology with ΩM = 0.3, ΩΛ = 0.7,
and H0 = 70 km s−1 Mpc−1; values quoted from other
relevant studies have been converted to this cosmology
where necessary. The lensing galaxy in iPTF16geu is lo-
cated at a redshift of z = 0.216 (Goobar et al. 2017), for
which 1′′ = 3.503 kpc. By contrast, the SN Ia is located
at z = 0.409 (Goobar et al. 2017).

show in the third column of Figure 3.
A faint point-

to the inability of the model S´ersics to exactly capture

2.1. Model Stellar Component
Armed with an improved understanding of the mor-
phology of the lensing galaxy, we repeated the procedure
described above with one minor reﬁnement. Unlike be-
fore, we ﬁxed the S´ersic index, n, of the outer (more
extended) component at n = 1 as is appropriate for an
exponential disk, while allowing (like before) its ellip-
ticity and position angle to freely vary in the ﬁt. For
the inner (more compact) component, we let its S´ersic
index, along with its ellipticity and position angle, to be
freely ﬁt, just like before. Once again, the centres of two
S´ersics are tied and solved in the ﬁtting process. Based
on the F814W image, for which the signal-to-noise ratio
is the highest, we ﬁnd n = 1.06±0.01 for the inner S´ersic
component. This S´ersic index is compatible with that of
a galactic bar: in a study of twenty nearby barred spiral
galaxies, Gadotti (2008) found that the S´ersic indices for
bars range from about 0.5 to 1.1. For the F475W and
F625W images, we ﬁxed n = 1.06 for the inner S´ersic
component ﬁtted to the lensing galaxy; in this way, for
all three ﬁlters, we ﬁnd closely similar values for all the
other parameters of the two S´ersic components ﬁtted to
the lensing galaxy.
The parameters of the model S´ersics thus ﬁtted to
the light morphology of the lensing galaxy (speciﬁcally,
in the F814W image) are listed in Table 1. Their com-
bined morphology is shown in the second column of Fig-
ure 3. These model S´ersics constitute the model stellar
component that we employed for the lensing galaxy in
iPTF16geu, as explained in more detail in Section 3.1.2.
2.2. Light Center
When constructing a lens model, it would seem sensi-
ble to localise the lens centre at the light centre of the
lensing galaxy. To estimate the uncertainty in the com-
mon centre of the two S´ersic proﬁles ﬁtted to the light
morphology of the lensing galaxy (see Section 2.1), we
performed Monte Carlo Markov Chain (MCMC) sim-
ulations for which we marginalised over all the other
free parameters of these two proﬁles. We found the un-
certainties in Right Ascension and Declination of this
common centre to both have approximately Gaussian
distributions, yielding a root-mean-square (rms) uncer-
tainty in this position of 3.3 mas (11.6 pc).
Tying the lens centre to the light centre implies conﬁ-
dence in relating the light centre to the dynamical cen-
tre of the lensing galaxy.
To determine whether the
light centre as inferred in the manner described above
likely coincides with the dynamical centre, we checked
whether the two S´ersic proﬁles ﬁtted to the light mor-
phology of the lensing galaxy indeed share the same cen-
tre. We therefore repeated the ﬁtting procedure as de-
scribed above (Section 2.1), but now freeing the centres
of the two S´ersic proﬁles to be individually ﬁtted. We
then performed MCMC simulations to evaluate the un-
certainty in the centre of each individual S´ersic proﬁle,
for which we marginalised over all the other free param-
eters of both proﬁles. The diﬀerent centres thus derived,

3. LENS MODELLING
We used the lensing algorithm glaﬁc (Oguri 2010,
2021) to construct all of our lens models.
This algo-
rithm implements a MCMC approach to explore toler-
ances in the lens model parameters given measurement
uncertainties in the lensing constraints.
Although we
performed MCMC simulations for all of our lens models
to explore tolerances in their parameters, in this paper
we present only the lens models that best ﬁt the SN Ia
image positions (i.e., leaving the lowest χ2), the sole con-
straints imposed. In this context, our primarily purpose
for performing MCMC simulations is to ensure that the
best-ﬁt lens model thus determined is not one that is
simply stuck at a local minimum in χ2.
3.1. Ingredients
To assess the extent to which including the model
stellar component for the lensing galaxy (Section 2.1)
improves upon the match in magniﬁcations if not also
positions of the SN Ia images, we consider lens models
that either exclude or include this model stellar com-
ponent.
Lens models that exclude the model stellar
component employ only a single mass component (one-
component lens model), whereas lens models that in-
clude the model stellar component employ two mass
components to explicitly represent stars and DM sep-
arately (two-component lens model).
For all the lens
models constructed, the redshift of the lensing galaxy
was set to z = 0.216 and that of the SN Ia to z = 0.409,
both as inferred from spectroscopy (Goobar et al. 2017).
The parameters that are ﬁxed or left free to be solved
in these lens models are summarised in Table 2, and de-
scribed in more detail next. Like nearly all previous lens
models constructed for iPTF16geu, we do not consider
any external shear in our lens modelling. A visual in-
spection reveals no bright galaxy or apparent concentra-
tion of faint galaxies in the immediate surroundings of
iPTF16geu, as shown in Figure 4. Possible hypothetical
complexities will be deferred to a future paper until we
understand how well relatively simple lens models (hav-
ing a minimal number of necessary ingredients) are able
to reproduce the multiply-lensed images in this system.

3.1.2. Two-Component Lens
The two-component lens model has separate compo-
nents representing stars and DM explicitly. For the stel-
lar component, we used the two model S´ersics designed
to ﬁt the light morphology of the lensing galaxy as de-
scribed in Section 2.1.
The parameters of this model
stellar component are listed in Table 1. The masses of
the individual S´ersic models were left to be freely ﬁt,
although with a prior imposed on their mass-to-light ra-
tio (M/L) over the range 0.5 ≤ M/L ≤ 4 as determined
for nearby spiral galaxies (Yoshino & Ichikawa 2008).
For the DM halo, we tried NFW, SIE, cSIE, or PL pro-
ﬁles as in the one-component lens model; the diﬀerent
DM halo proﬁles considered allow for deviations from a
NFW proﬁle, induced for instance by gravitational in-
teractions between the DM halo and the baryonic com-
ponent of the galaxy. Like in the one-component lens
model, the ellipsoidal shape and mass of the DM halo
are left to be freely ﬁt.
In the lens modelling, we tie the centres of the model
stellar component and DM halo, but let this common
centre be freely ﬁt. A close agreement with the light
centre of the lensing galaxy, for which uncertainties in
determining this centre is discussed in Section 2.2, would
then provide conﬁdence on the validity of the lens model.
As an instructive comparison, we also explore lens mod-
els where we localise (within measurement uncertainties)
the lens centre at the light centre inferred by tying the
centres of the two S´ersic proﬁles ﬁtted to the light mor-
phology of the lensing galaxy (see details in Section 2.2).
We note that the two model S´ersics – capturing, re-
spectively, light from the stellar bar and stellar disk – do
not explicitly capture light from the bluish spiral arms.
These arms appear to extend outwards from the stellar
bar, ﬁrst appearing at radii beyond the SN Ia images and
the ER. Likely represent slight enhancements in surface
mass density over the inter-arm region, we do not antic-
ipate these spiral arms to signiﬁcantly aﬀect the lensing
potential at the locations of the SN Ia images.
3.2. Constraints
In this paper, we constrain our smooth lens models
solely by the positions of the four SN Ia images, just
like in all previous smooth lens models for iPTF16geu
that reportedly are able reproduce these positions (see
Section 1). Adopting this approach carries the implicit
assumption that any ingredients not considered in lens
models can leave discrepancies in magniﬁcations while
not appreciably altering the positions of multiply-lensed
images. The most obvious such ingredients left out of
smooth lens models are discrete stars and sub-halos
(which, when they contain stars, constitute satellite
galaxies) associated with the lensing galaxy, both of
which can induce additional lensing.
Schechter & Wambsganss (2002); Keeton et al. (2003);
Kochanek & Dalal (2003) have shown that the addition
of small-scale structures (whether stars or sub-halos)
to smooth lens models tend to suppress negative-parity
images (located inside the critical curve) more often
than they amplify positive-parity images (located out-
side the critical curve).
Furthermore, Palencia et al.
(2024) also show that stellar micro-lensing is much more
likely to suppress rather than amplify negative-parity
AASTeX v6.3.1 Sample article
9
images, and to amplify rather than suppress positive-
parity images.
All lens models constructed thus far
for iPTF16geu predict Img 1 (the brightest) and Img 3
(formally the dimmest) to be located inside the critical
curve and therefore have negative parity, and Img 2 and
Img 4 to be located outside the critical curve and there-
fore have positive parity – including those presented in
this paper. Under the notion that magniﬁcation anoma-
lies can be resolved by including stellar micro-lensing
and/or DM sub-halos, lens modellers would therefore
prefer smooth lens models to over-predict the magniﬁca-
tions of Img 1 (the highest magniﬁcation) and img 3 (for-
mally the lowest magniﬁcation) more dramatically than
they under-predict the magniﬁcations of Img 2 (second
highest magniﬁcation) and Img 4 (formally second low-
est magniﬁcation); in the speciﬁc case of stellar micro-
lensing, for smooth lens models to over- rather than
under-predict the magniﬁcations of Img 1 and Img 3, and
to under- rather than over-predict the magniﬁcations of
Img 2 and Img 4.
In our work, we deliberately choose to be agnostic
about whether our smooth lens models over- or under-
predict the magniﬁcations of the SN Ia images inside
or outside the critical curve.
In so doing, we are al-
lowing for other potential causes of lensing anomalies
apart from just stellar micro-lensing or sub-halos. For
instance, if DM comprises ultra-light bosons in a Bose-
Einstein condensate, then pervasive density modulations
in DM generated by the quantum interference of such
particles will inherently induce apparent lensing anoma-
lies (Amruth et al. 2023). Although such density modu-
lations also suppress negative-parity images more often
than they amplify positive-parity images formed near
critical curves, this skewness is expected to be less pro-
nounced than for either stellar micro-lensing or sub-
halos; the key diﬀerence being that quantum interfer-
ence among ultra-light cold DM particles can just as
frequently produce under- as over-dense ﬂuctuations in
surface mass densities, whereas stars and DM sub-halos
can only produce over-dense ﬂuctuations in surface mass
densities. As a consequence, it is crucial that we do not
pre-dispose ourselves to a particular smooth lens model
so as to deliberately engineer a preferred solution to lens-
ing anomalies.
3.2.1. SN Ia Image Positions
To accurately determine the positions of the SN Ia
images for use as constraints on lens models, we ﬁrst
subtracted the post-SN Ia image of Figure 1b from the
SN Ia image of Figure 1a. The resulting image therefore
contains the SN Ia images alone. We then ﬁt a Moﬀat
proﬁle to each SN Ia image in the F814W ﬁlter using the
package IMFIT (Erwin 2015), where the signal-to-noise
ratio is the highest, to determine their individual po-
sitions. To determine their positional uncertainties, we
performed MCMC simulations (implemented in IMFIT)
to sample values around the best-ﬁt Moﬀat parameters
of the four SN Ia images.
In this way, we found po-
sitional uncertainties for each SN Ia image to have an
approximately Gaussian distribution in both Right As-
cension and Declination.
Table 3 lists the positions and associated positional
uncertainties of all four SN Ia images. Their rms posi-
tional uncertainties range from ∼0.4 mas for the bright-
est SN Ia image (Img 1) to ∼2.3 mas for the dimmest
SN Ia images (both Img 3 and Img 4). These uncertain-
ties are much smaller than that usually attainable for
multiply-lensed QSOs – except for measurements at ra-
dio wavelengths using Very Long Baseline Interferome-
try (VLBI) – as we as can subtract both the ER and light
from the lensing galaxy before determining the position
of each SN Ia image.
Such high positional precisions
can potentially reveal position anomalies in lens mod-
els, such as those found among multiply-lensed systems
imaged using VLBI (Hartley et al. 2019; Biggs & et al.
2004; Spingola & et al. 2018). The SN Ia image magniﬁ-
cations listed in Table 3 are those determined by Dhawan
et al. (2020) after correcting for dust extinction imposed
by the foreground lensing galaxy.
For the purpose of
this paper, these magniﬁcations are used solely to check
for magniﬁcation anomalies among the lens models con-
structed.

3.2.2. Velocity Dispersion of Lensing Galaxy
A useful check on the mass of the lensing galaxy in
iPTF16geu is provided by measurements of its stel-
10
lar velocity dispersion.
Cano et al. (2018) observed
iPTF16geu with the VLT+XSHOOTER at 18.6 days
and 27.1 days after the SN Ia peaked in brightness. In
both observations, they employed a slit having a width
of 0.′′9 (at optical and near-IR wavelengths) placed at a
position angle of +86○ across the centre of the system.
Fortuitously, the slit placement is quite closely parallel
to the stellar bar, which dominates the light of the lens-
ing galaxy, and is wide enough to encompass the entire
bar. Johansson et al. (2021) report an analysis of the
spectra at rest-frame optical wavelengths, from which
they determine a stellar (radial) velocity dispersion of
σ = 129 ± 4kms−1.
A direct comparison of the measured stellar veloc-
ity dispersion with that implied by lens models would
require computing, for a given lens model, what stel-
lar velocity dispersion would be measured over the slit
aperture given a spatial dependence in stellar velocity
dispersion (which is determined by the gravitational po-
tential of stars and DM combined) that is weighted by
the spatial dependence in light intensity. Analytical cal-
culations are tractable only by assuming spherical sym-
metry, as performed by M¨ortsell et al. (2020) for their
lens models of iPTF16geu. The procedure adopted in
our work is described in Section 5.
4. ASSESSING LENS MODELS VERSUS
EVALUATING LENSING ANOMALIES
Lens models are usually assessed by evaluating diﬀer-
ences in both positions and (relative or absolute) magni-
ﬁcations between the predicted and observed multiply-
lensed images. Such diﬀerences, if any, provide clues to
ingredients that can be added into lens models to alle-
viate, if not resolve, lensing anomalies. In Figure 5, we
depict the diﬀerent situations that can be encountered
when constructing lens models, focussing on just one im-
age counterpart among several belonging to a multiply-
lensed system. The black × indicates the nominal po-
sition of this image counterpart, with the dashed black
circle indicating the 1σ measurement uncertainty in its
position. The magniﬁcation of this image, and the 1σ
measurement uncertainty in its magniﬁcation, is given
by µobs. The position predicted by the lens model for
this image counterpart is indicated by a red +, and its
predicted magniﬁcation by µpred.
In Figure 5a, both the predicted image position and
magniﬁcation are in satisfactory agreement – deﬁned
here to be within the 1σ measurement uncertainties –
with the observed image. If repeated in the same man-
ner for all image counterparts, this lens model would
be assessed to leave no lensing anomalies. By contrast,
in Figure 5b, while the predicted and observed image
positions are in satisfactory agreement, the predicted
and observed image magniﬁcations are not. This ﬁgure
depicts the situation reported for all lens models con-
structed thus far for iPTF16geu, leading both M¨ortsell
et al. (2020) and Diego et al. (2022) to invoke stellar
micro-lensing for resolving the magniﬁcation anomalies
left by lens models for this system.
As we shall now explain, evaluations of lensing anoma-
lies do not necessarily provide accurate assessments of
whether lens models are actually able to reproduce the
observed properties of multiply-lensed images. Before
concluding that magniﬁcation (or, for that matter, po-
sition) anomalies imply an inherent deﬁciency in lens
models, it should be kept in mind that the predicted im-
age positions and therefore also magniﬁcations depend
not only on the lens model but also on the assumed
intrinsic sky position of the background lensed source.
Altering the source position could, potentially, bring to
satisfactory agreement the predicted image magniﬁca-
tion at the newly-predicted image position: e.g., in Fig-
ure 5b, to any position within the green contours where
the magniﬁcation predicted by the lens model is in satis-
factory agreement with the observed magniﬁcation. All
viable lens models constructed thus far for iPTF16geu
have been constrained solely by the SN Ia image posi-
tions, and are therefore optimised to leave the smallest
dispersion in positions between the predicted and ob-
served images.
As a consequence, simply by altering
the assumed source position, it is possible that a bet-
ter overall match in magniﬁcations can be achieved at
the price of a larger dispersion in positions, yet leaving
in satisfactory agreement all the predicted and observed
image positions.

Figure 5c–d leave a position anomaly for the image coun-
terpart depicted. Both these situations exemplify that
found by Diego et al. (2022) when using the positions of
all four SN Ia images and the magniﬁcations of just Img 2
and Img 4 as constraints (the latter two images deemed
to be less likely subjected to stellar micro-lensing), for
which the lens model thus constructed leave position
anomalies for both Img 1 and Img 3. Despite leaving dif-
ferent types of lensing anomalies, the lens model in Fig-

giving rise to a diﬀerent predicted position and magni-
ﬁcation for the image counterpart depicted. Similarly,
the lens model in Figure 5c (evaluated to leave a posi-

to leave no lensing anomaly), except that the assumed

are able to reproduce the positions and magniﬁcations of
the SN Ia images, rather than simply evaluating lensing
anomalies – which also depend on the assumed source
position – left by lens models. In this regard, when lens
models leave position anomalies, it is not meaningful –
from the viewpoint of assessing lens models – to com-

of the observed image, as we would then be comparing
magniﬁcations at two diﬀerent positions. For example,
in Figure 5d, the magniﬁcation of the predicted image

what lens models predict from the assumed source posi-

the 1σ positional error circle of each image counterpart.
This diagnostic serves as a reminder that loosening the
overall agreement in image positions can potentially al-

tively, allowing for an especially large position anomaly
at one image counterpart, perhaps induced by a DM sub-
halo, can potentially alleviate both position and magni-
ﬁcation anomalies at other image counterparts.

Figure 6 provides a simple visual assessment of how
well the diﬀerent lens models are able to reproduce the
SN Ia image positions and magniﬁcations. In the ﬁrst
column, the solid bars indicate individual diﬀerences be-
tween the predicted and observed SN Ia image positions
– given an inferred intrinsic sky position for the SN Ia
that minimises these positional diﬀerences. The horizon-
tal dashed lines indicate the 1σ measurement uncertain-
ties in the observed SN Ia image positions. Satisfactory
agreement between the predicted and observed positions
implies that the lens model has correctly captured –
within observational uncertainties – the ﬁrst derivative
of the true lensing potential at the location of a given
SN Ia image.
As can be easily seen from Figure 6, all the one-
component lens models leave position anomalies for
Img 2–4, deﬁned here as diﬀerences between the pre-
dicted and observed SN Ia image positions larger than
the 1σ positional measurement uncertainties. Previous
lens models reported in the literature (see Section 1) do
not specify how closely they reproduce the the SN Ia im-
age positions, but our results suggest signiﬁcant discrep-
ancies when measurement uncertainties for the SN Ia im-
age positions are properly evaluated as described in Sec-
tion 3.2. By contrast, all the two-component lens models
that incorporate a power-law density proﬁle (i.e., SIE,
cSIE, or PL) for the DM halo of the lensing galaxy repro-
duce the positions of all the SN Ia images to near-perfect
agreement.
Even the two-component lens model that
incorporates a NFW DM halo provides a far superior
match in SN Ia image positions compared to the corre-
sponding one-component lens model that employs just a
single NFW proﬁle, leaving position anomalies for only a
single image (Img 2) slightly larger than 1σ. In this sit-
uation, simply altering the intrinsic sky position of the
SN Ia could potentially resolve the position anomaly for
Img 2 while leaving no position anomalies at the other
images, at the price of a larger dispersion in positions
between the predicted and observed images. Lens mod-
els that incorporate the model stellar component along
with a DM halo therefore alleviates, if not fully resolves,
position anomalies left by all the one-component lens
models constructed for iPTF16geu.
The second (middle) column of Figure 6 depicts how
closely the predicted image magniﬁcations agree with
the observed SN Ia image magniﬁcations.
The darker
solid bars indicate the magniﬁcations of the individual
SN Ia images, with error bars corresponding to the ±1σ
measurement uncertainties in their magniﬁcations. The
hatched bars indicate the predicted image magniﬁca-
tions. Provided that the predicted and observed posi-
tions of a given SN Ia image agree (to within the 1σ po-
sitional measurement uncertainty), overlap between the
predicted and observed magniﬁcations (to within mea-
surements uncertainties in the observed magniﬁcation)
indicates that the lens model correctly captures – within
observational uncertainties – both the ﬁrst and second
derivative of the true lensing potential at the location of
that SN Ia image.
All the one-component lens models, which leave posi-
tion anomalies for three of the four SN Ia images, predict
closely comparable image magniﬁcations that are well
within a factor of 2. The same has been found in previ-
ous studies employing only a single mass component in
lens models for iPTF16geu (see Section 1). Furthermore,
the predicted image magniﬁcations can be very diﬀerent
from the observed SN Ia image magniﬁcations, especially
for Img 1 (the most highly magniﬁed) whereby the pre-
dicted image magniﬁcation is a factor of ≥ 3 either too
low or too high. By contrast, the two-component lens
models yield a larger range of predicted image magniﬁ-
cations, diﬀering by at least a factor of 2. Furthermore,
except for the two-component lens model incorporating
a NFW DM halo, the remaining – all incorporating a
power-law (either SIE, cSIE, or PL) DM halo – predict
image magniﬁcations that are relatively close to those
observed.
The eﬀect of adding the model stellar component is
shown in the third (last) column of Figure 6.
In this
column, the positions of the observed SN Ia images are
indicated by �, superposed on an image that shows the
SN Ia images only (i.e., after subtracting the post-SN Ia
image), to be compared against the positions predicted
by the lens model as indicated by ×.
The centre of
each lens model is located at the origin. The smaller
of the two ellipses (sometimes so small as to appear as
a dot) traces the predicted radial critical curve, whereas
the larger ellipse traces the predicted tangential criti-
cal curve.
All the observed SN Ia images are located
close to the tangential critical curve, such that small
changes in the shape of the critical curve – and hence
small changes in the displacement of the observed SN Ia
images from this curve – can result in relatively large
changes in their predicted magniﬁcations. Whereas the
critical curves of the one-component lens model are pure
ellipses, the critical curves of the two-component lens
models (most noticeably their tangential critical curves)
are quite boxy (shape that is between an ellipse and a
rectangle). The model stellar component therefore in-
troduces signiﬁcant changes to the shape of the critical
curve, bringing the tangential critical curve closer to or
away from the predicted SN Ia image positions thus al-
tering also their predicted magniﬁcations.

5.2. Lens and Light Centres
16
As mentioned at the start of Section 5, both the one-
component and two-component lens models have cen-
tres that are shifted by nearly the same distance and at
the nearly the same position angle from the light cen-
tre. The latter is inferred by ﬁtting two S´ersics proﬁles,
sharing a common centre, to the light morphology of the
lensing galaxy (see Section 2.2).
We now check whether restricting the lens centre to
the light centre (within measurement uncertainties) al-
ters how well the SN Ia image positions and magniﬁca-
tions can be reproduced. To make this check, we solved
for a new two-component lens model in which the cen-
tres of the model stellar component and SIE DM halo
are tied together (like before), but for which a Gaus-
sian prior having σ = 3.3mas (11.6 pc; the positional
measurement of the ﬁtted light centre) is placed on the
lens centre.
The best-ﬁt lens model requires the lens
centre to be oﬀset by just 4.6 mas (14.4 pc) from the
nominal light centre. The image positions and magni-
ﬁcations predicted by this lens model is shown in the
upper row of Figure 8. As can be seen, this lens model
leave position anomalies for all the SN Ia images, and
predict image magniﬁcations very diﬀerent from those
observed. The inability of this lens model to meet even
the positional constraints imposed reinforces our argu-
ment that the inferred light centre does not mark the
dynamical centre of the lensing galaxy.
For the sake of completeness, we also tested whether
allowing the DM halo to have a diﬀerent centre from the
model stellar component, itself restricted to positions
within measurement uncertainties of the light centre,
better reproduces the SN Ia image positions and mag-
niﬁcations.
The resulting best-ﬁt lens model requires
the two centres to diﬀer by 86.5mas (301 pc). As shown
in the lower row of Figure 8, this lens model leaves po-
sition anomalies for Img 2-4 and predicts closely similar
magniﬁcations for all the images – in clear contradiction
with observations.

5.4. Necessity of DM Halo
Given the spatial complexity of the model stellar com-
ponent and its importance for reproducing the SN Ia im-
age positions and magniﬁcations (to the degree possible)
in iPTF16geu, the reader may well wonder whether the
inclusion of a DM halo is at all necessary in lens models
for iPTF16geu. Retaining only the model stellar com-
ponent, we newly solved for the masses of the stellar bar
and stellar disk so as to construct lens models that min-
imise, as before, the dispersion between the predicted
and observed SN Ia image positions. Freeing the cen-
tre of this modiﬁed lens model, we now ﬁnd its centre
to be displaced by 0.′′112 (408 pc) from the light centre,
signiﬁcantly larger than in all the two-component lens
models. We also constructed a version of this modiﬁed
lens model in which the lens centre is ﬁxed to the light
centre. As shown in Figure 10, both these modiﬁed lens
models predict image conﬁgurations that are completely
diﬀerent than the observed SN Ia image conﬁgurations.
Clearly, in addition to a stellar component, a DM halo
is required in lens models for iPTF16geu.

SN Ia image magniﬁcations. Moreover, lens models that

In this paper, through a simple procedure to subtract-
ing the ER from the post-SN Ia image (Section 2), we
reveal the lensing galaxy to be a barred spiral (Fig. 3).
We ﬁt two elliptical S´ersic proﬁles (parameters listed in
Table 1) to the light morphology of the lensing galaxy
to serve as a model stellar component for this galaxy.
For the Dark Matter (DM) halo, we employ commonly-
used density proﬁles corresponding to (i) Navarro-Frenk-
White (NFW), (ii) singular isothermal ellipsoid (SIE);
(iii) cored isothermal ellipsoid (cSIE); and (iv) power-
law (PL), which equates to isothermal when the density,
ρ, decreases with radius, r, as ρ ∝ 1/r2. The diﬀerent
choices considered for the DM halo allow for deviations
from a NFW proﬁle, induced for instance by gravita-
tional interactions between the DM halo and baryons
– resulting in a radial density proﬁle diﬀerent from a
NFW proﬁle at the locations of the SN Ia images. In
this way, we also can directly compare how well the
two-component lens models reproduce the positions and
magniﬁcations of the SN Ia images with the types of lens
models previously reported employing only a single mass
component (SIE, cSIE, or PL). All the lens models thus
constructed are constrained by the four SN Ia image po-
sitions only, and are therefore optimised to minimise the
dispersion between the predicted and observed images.
The results for all the best-ﬁt lens models are shown
in Figure 6. The single-component lens model, for which
the model stellar component is omitted, all leave posi-
tion anomalies for two or more images, and predict im-
20
age magniﬁcation spanning a relatively narrow range of
less than a factor of 2 (as found for all such lens models
previously reported). By contrast, the two-component
lens models that incorporate both the model stellar com-
ponent and a power-law density proﬁle for the DM halo
(whether SIE, cSIE, or PL) near-perfectly reproduce the
observed SN Ia image positions, and predict a broader
range of image magniﬁcations in closer overall agreement
with the observed SN Ia image magniﬁcations. Even the
two component lens model that incorporate a NFW DM
halo leave only a 1.0σ tension between the predicted and
observed positions for just one SN Ia image, although
predicting image magniﬁcations that are all very dif-
ferent from those observed. The lens model that most
closely reproduces the SN Ia image magniﬁcations is that
incorporating the model stellar component along with a
SIE DM halo (see Figure 7): this model near-perfectly
reproduces the SN Ia image positions, satisfactorily re-
produces the magniﬁcation of Img 2 (the second bright-
est image), leaves only a 2.2σ tension between the pre-
dicted and observed magniﬁcation of Img 1 (the bright-
est image), but signiﬁcantly over-predicts the magniﬁca-
tions of Img 3–4 (the two comparably dimmest images)
by factors of ∼2–3.
We emphasise that evaluations of lensing anomalies do
not necessarily provide accurate assessments of whether
lens models are actually able to reproduce the observed
properties of multiply-lensed images – as the predicted
image positions and hence also magniﬁcation depend not
only on the lens model, but also on the (unknown) as-
sumed intrinsic sky position of the lensed source.
To
separate the predictions of the lens model from the as-
sumed source position, we advocate plotting the pre-
dicted range of magniﬁcations over the 1σ positional er-
ror circle of the individual image positions, as shown in
Figure 6. In this way, we ﬁnd that, for our preferred lens
model incorporating the model stellar component along
with a SIE DM halo, altering the source position cannot
bring any of the three images having discrepant mag-
niﬁcations to satisfactory agreement with the observed
SN Ia image magniﬁcations.
Although closing the gap between model predictions
and observations, the work described in this paper serves
as just a starting point for future studies to better eval-
uate what lensing anomalies, if any, are left by smooth
lens models for iPTF16geu. In this paper, the lens mod-
els constructed are constrained solely by the SN Ia image
positions: this approach presupposes that any missing
ingredients in lens models leave discrepancies in mag-
niﬁcations while not appreciably altering the positions
of multiply-lensed images. One such possible ingredient
is stellar microlensing; as its eﬀect on image magniﬁca-
tion depends on the underlying smooth lens model, all
previous studies implementing stellar microlensing (Ya-
halomi et al. 2017; M¨ortsell et al. 2020; Diego et al.
2022) need to be re-evaluated by using more exacting
smooth lens models for iPTF16geu. For instance, our
preferred lens model over-predicts the magniﬁcations of
Img 3–4, whereas lens models incorporating stellar mi-
crolensing predict Img 4 to be much more likely to be
further ampliﬁed rather than being suppressed (thus
providing an even poorer match with observations) –
although Img 3 is much more likely to be suppressed
(bringing it to closer agreement with observations) – by
stellar microlensing (see explanation in Section 3.2).
Future studies should also implement constraints pro-
vided by the SN Ia image magniﬁcations as well as the
full morphology of the ER (comprising the multiply-
lensed images of the SN Ia host galaxy), the subject of
Paper II in this series. Such studies are necessary to fully
assess the nature and magnitude of lensing anomalies
left by smooth lens models for iPTF16geu – and, in turn,
for evaluating what additional ingredients are needed to
resolve these lensing anomalies. If stellar micro-lensing
proves unable to fully resolve magniﬁcation anomalies
left by smooth lens models, or a more detailed examina-
tion of smooth lens models leave position anomalies, we
would then need to explore other possibilities such as:
(i) DM sub-halos, as are predicted in abundance around
massive galaxies by cosmological simulations employing
ultra-massive and cold DM particles; or (ii) pervasive
density modulations in DM halos (along with a sup-
pression of low-mass DM sub-halos), as predicted by
cosmological simulations employing ultra-light bosons in
a Bose-Einstein condensate. The type (position and/or
magniﬁcation) of lensing anomalies, and the magnitude
of such anomalies, can therefore have important implica-
tions for the nature of DM: in lieu of a direct detection in
laboratory experiments, the nature of and resolution to
lensing anomalies oﬀers a promising approach to distin-
guish between ultra-massive particles such as WIMPs
and ultra-light particles such as Axions or Axions-like
particles for DM (e.g., see Amruth et al. 2023) – all the
leading contenders for DM. The issues raised are the
subjects of future papers in this series.
